In this study, I'll ask you to tell me about a decision that you haven't made yet, but you will make soon. The decision should be one you feel is weighty enough to be worth real thought. Ideally it's also a decision such that, by 1 month from today, it's likely that you'll have made the decision and gotten some idea of how well your choice worked out for you. You might consider a decision situation with only two options (such as whether to take or not take a certain action) or a situation with several options.

Here are a lot of examples of the kind of decision you might use for this study (but, of course, you can still use something different):

  • Deciding whether to quit a job, or deciding which of several job offers to take
  • Deciding whether to start a new business venture, or whether to give up on an existing one
  • Deciding whether to start or drop out of school, or deciding which school to attend
  • Deciding whether to join or leave a religion
  • Deciding who to vote for
  • Deciding whether to come out as LGBT to someone you know
  • Deciding whether to ask somebody on a date, marry, or break up
  • Deciding whether to have a child
  • Deciding where to move, or what home to rent or buy
  • Deciding on expensive purchases, such as a car
  • Medical decisions, such whether to get a vaccine, whether to undergo surgery, or whether to seek help for a mental-health problem

Approximately, what is the latest date by which you think you'll have made the decision and be able to say whether you're happy with the choice you made?

Now your task is to represent your alternatives (options) and their pros and cons in a certain format. The idea is to represent each alternative with a set of "criteria"; each criterion is a property of the option or its likely outcome. For examine, if you're choosing between job offers, a criterion could be Salary. You can have as many criteria as you want. The important thing is to have enough criteria to capture what makes options appealing or unappealing to you, but not have so many that you get overwhelmedâ€”if you don't care about something, don't use it as a criterion. I suggest aiming for 2 to 7.

Each criterion has several possible values, called "levels", and the levels are ordered with the worst first and the best last. So you might give Salary the levels Low, Medium, and High. You could just as well change these levels (e.g., adding Very high after High), or you could use dollar amounts instead of words. The important thing is that you have enough levels to describe your alternatives, and later levels are better than earlier ones. So if two jobs have different salaries in dollars, but the difference is too small to affect your decision at all, you should use the same level of Salary for both alternatives.
